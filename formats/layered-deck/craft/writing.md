# Writing Standards — Layered Deck

These standards govern how individual slides are written — below the argument level, above the execution level. They apply at Layer 2.5 (Content Normalization) or Layer 2 when no separate content normalization step exists. They are cross-cutting: they apply regardless of which layer you are working in.

---

## Presenter-First Title Standard

A slide title must be clear enough that a presenter who loses their train of thought mid-delivery can read it and know exactly what to say next. This rules out metaphors, abstract constructions, and wordplay — not because they are wrong, but because they require decoding under pressure.

**Test:** Can someone read the title cold and immediately derive the slide's message? If not, simplify.

**In practice:** Prefer plain, active, speakable phrasing. A title that states the claim directly ("Your strategy drives every design decision") outperforms one that implies it ("Your Strategy, Made Tangible") when the presenter's cognitive load is high.

Titles are not headlines — they are lifelines. Write them for the moment the presenter needs them most.

---

## Content Slide Formula

For argument slides — those making a specific claim, as distinct from index or transition slides — the default structure is:

1. **Title** — the one thing the slide is about, stated directly. 4–8 words. Speakable under pressure. No metaphors required.
2. **Hero paragraph** — 2–3 sentences. Owns the main argument. This is where the "why it matters" lives. The hero must be able to stand alone.
3. **3 power bullets** — one per distinct dimension. Each bullet covers ground the hero does not.

---

## Bullet Distinctness Rule

The three bullets must cover different dimensions of the slide's claim — typically: what we bring in (input), how we work (process), and what comes out (output). If any two bullets could be merged without losing meaning, one needs to be replaced.

**The test:** Read the hero, then each bullet in turn. Any bullet that restates the hero at a different level of abstraction is redundant and must be replaced.

---

## Patterns to Avoid

**"Not just X, Y" constructions.** A recognizable AI writing pattern that signals unnatural phrasing to a native reader. If contrast is needed, restructure to lead with the positive claim directly — use "X and Y" or reorder the sentence so the affirmative version is the main clause.
